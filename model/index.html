The Unified Model

The Agency Wheel

Agency sits at the center. The Thought–Feeling–Action loop is the mechanism. The Line determines direction. Every leadership cluster lives in the outer ring — all of it powered by the single pivot of conscious choice.

The Oz Principle

Above the Line.
Below the Line.

The most important leadership tool is deceptively simple: a horizontal line. At every moment, in every circumstance, a leader's thoughts, feelings, and actions are oriented either above or below it. And the choice is always available — regardless of circumstances.

See It

Acknowledge reality clearly, without minimizing or catastrophizing. Accurate perception is the first act of agency.

Own It

Accept your role in the situation — not with guilt, but with power. Ownership is the source of influence.

Solve It

Turn attention from what happened to what's next. Creativity and resourcefulness only operate above the line.

Do It

Execute. The loop from thought to feeling to action completes here — with committed, accountable follow-through.

— The Line —

Agency is not the absence of difficulty. It is the choice, made in the space between stimulus and response, to orient upward.

Blame

Transfer responsibility outward. The circumstances, the team, the weather, the organization — anyone but self.

Avoid

Disconnect from the problem. Deny it exists, minimize its importance, or change the subject.

Wait & See

Defer action indefinitely. Passivity in the face of a problem that requires engagement is its own choice.

Hide

Conceal mistakes, information, or uncertainty. The loop closes here — in contraction rather than growth.

"Everything can be taken from a man but one thing: the last of the human freedoms — to choose one's attitude in any given set of circumstances."

Frankl developed logotherapy in the Nazi concentration camps — the most extreme possible test of agency. His conclusion was not inspirational abstraction. It was empirical: meaning-making and agency persist even when all external freedom is stripped away. The Oz Principle begins where Frankl ends.

The genius of Connors, Smith & Hickman is the operationalization of Frankl's philosophy into a diagnostic tool that works in real time. The Line is visible. The BASH pattern (Blame, Abdicate, Shuffle, Hide) is nameable. And naming it — "I'm below the line right now" — is itself an act of above-the-line consciousness.

The interpretive layer. How you make meaning of what happens. Thoughts are the most accessible entry point for agency — you can observe a thought, question it, and choose a different one. CBT, mindfulness, cognitive reframing, and neuroleadership all work at this level.

The visible output. The point where inner life meets the world. But actions also shape thoughts and feelings — behavioral activation, the "act as if" principle, and adaptive leadership all leverage action as an entry point into the loop, not merely its product.

Above the line: the loop runs upward — generative thoughts produce positive emotion that drives constructive action, which confirms and strengthens the original thought. Below the line: the same loop, running in reverse — producing self-reinforcing cycles of victimhood, stagnation, and contraction. Agency is the hinge.

01
PHILOSOPHICAL FOUNDATION
Viktor Frankl
Man's Search for Meaning · 1946  ·  Logotherapy
"Between stimulus and response there is a space. In that space is our power to choose our response. In our response lies our growth and our freedom."
A Viennese psychiatrist, Frankl lost his family and spent three years in Nazi concentration camps including Auschwitz. Rather than destroying his belief in human agency, the experience confirmed it. He observed that even under total deprivation, some prisoners retained the freedom to choose their attitude — and that this inner freedom was unkillable. He called the resulting therapy logotherapy: healing through meaning-making.
What it gives the model The irreducible philosophical bedrock. If agency survives Auschwitz, no leader in any organization can claim their circumstances prevent them from choosing above the line. This is the argument that silences every excuse — not with judgment, but with evidence.

02
ORGANIZATIONAL TOOL
Connors, Smith & Hickman
The Oz Principle · 1994  ·  Partners In Leadership
"People and organizations that operate above the line get results. Those that operate below the line make excuses."
Using the Wizard of Oz as its central metaphor — Dorothy had the power to go home all along — Connors, Smith & Hickman argue that individuals and organizations consistently underestimate their own agency and overestimate the power of circumstances. The See It–Own It–Solve It–Do It framework translates Frankl's philosophy into a four-step organizational practice. The Line becomes a shared language, a real-time diagnostic, and a cultural standard.
What it gives the model The practical bridge from philosophy to daily organizational life. The Line gives the model a shared vocabulary that groups can use in real time: "I'm below the line right now." That self-naming is itself an act of above-the-line consciousness — and it is learnable, repeatable, and transferable.

03
DAILY ARCHITECTURE
Stephen R. Covey
The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People · 1989
"Between stimulus and response, man has the freedom to choose. Proactivity is the habit of taking responsibility for your own life — acting rather than being acted upon."
Covey cites Frankl as the direct inspiration for Habit 1 — Be Proactive — and builds his entire framework on the same foundation. His Circle of Influence vs. Circle of Concern is the most teachable operationalization of above-the-line thinking ever devised. Reactive language ("I can't," "I have to," "If only") vs. proactive language ("I choose," "I will," "I prefer") makes the invisible thought-level choice audible. Covey's framework shows that agency is not a single moment — it is a daily practice structured across seven interdependent habits.
What it gives the model The habitual architecture. Frankl identifies the space of freedom; Covey shows how to live in it every day. The Circle of Influence gives participants a concrete, immediately applicable tool for identifying where their agency actually operates — and where they're wasting energy on what they cannot control.

04
SCIENTIFIC VALIDATION
Albert Bandura
Self-Efficacy: The Exercise of Control · 1997  ·  Social Cognitive Theory
"People are both producers and products of their environment. Human agency is not a ghost in the machine — it is the machine."
One of the most cited psychologists in history, Bandura spent five decades building the scientific case for human agency. Self-efficacy — the belief that your actions can produce specific outcomes — is not optimism. It is the empirically measurable cognitive prerequisite for choosing above the line. His research shows that people with high self-efficacy persist longer, recover faster, set higher goals, and experience less anxiety. His 2001 paper on human agency in American Psychologist formally argues that humans are producers of their environment, not merely products — the scientific statement of the Agency Model's core claim.
What it gives the model Scientific credibility and measurability. Self-efficacy can be assessed, developed, and tracked. It tells us why some people find agency more accessible than others — not because of fixed traits, but because of belief systems shaped by experience. Wilderness challenges are among the most powerful self-efficacy builders available.

05
COGNITIVE PREREQUISITE
Carol S. Dweck
Mindset: The New Psychology of Success · 2006  ·  Stanford University
"In a fixed mindset, students believe their basic abilities are fixed traits. In a growth mindset, students believe their abilities can be developed. This view creates a love of learning and resilience essential to accomplishment."
Dweck's decades of research at Stanford identified the most fundamental cognitive variable in human development: the belief about whether ability is fixed or growable. A fixed mindset — "I am what I am" — is the cognitive architecture of the below-the-line trap. It makes agency feel unavailable because effort is seen as evidence of inadequacy rather than the path to mastery. A growth mindset — "I can develop" — is the cognitive soil in which agency grows. Critically, Dweck showed that the mindset itself can be changed — that the belief in changeability is what produces change.
What it gives the model The cognitive prerequisite map. Before you can consistently choose above the line, you need to believe the choice matters — that your effort will change something. A fixed mindset forecloses agency before it can be exercised. Dweck shows how to develop the belief structure that makes the model work. Wilderness failure and recovery is one of the most powerful growth-mindset interventions available.

06
THE DOWNSIDE MAP
Martin E. P. Seligman
Learned Helplessness · 1967  ·  Learned Optimism · 1991  ·  Penn University
"Learned helplessness is the giving-up reaction, the quitting response that follows from the belief that whatever you do doesn't matter."
Seligman's foundational research began with a disturbing discovery: dogs exposed to uncontrollable shocks stopped trying to escape even when escape became possible. The pattern transferred to humans. When people repeatedly experience outcomes they cannot control, they stop believing that their actions matter — and this belief persists even when the environment changes. This is the clinical and neurological picture of being permanently below the line. His later work on explanatory style — how people explain bad events using the 3 P's (Permanent, Pervasive, Personal) — maps precisely onto the T·F·A loop running in reverse. Learned Optimism is the recovery program: reconnecting action to outcome.
What it gives the model The downside map and the clinical stakes. Seligman shows that victimhood is not merely a bad habit — it can become a neurological and psychological trap that requires deliberate intervention to escape. He also shows the mechanism of recovery: accurate attribution of outcomes to actions. The wilderness, where cause and effect are immediate and undeniable, is one of the most powerful anti-helplessness environments available.
T + F + A — the full loop, running below the line

07
THE STRATEGIC BRIDGE
Clayton M. Christensen
How Will You Measure Your Life? · 2012  ·  Harvard Business School
"The allocation of your personal time, energy, and talent ultimately shapes your life's strategy — whether you mean it to or not. The danger is in not deciding."
The most influential business theorist of the last thirty years — the man who gave the world Disruptive Innovation and The Innovator's Dilemma — spent the final decade of his life applying those same frameworks to the question of personal purpose. Christensen argued that the same forces that cause great companies to fail cause great lives to fail: the dangerous comfort of optimization, the marginal-cost reasoning that justifies small compromises, and the misreading of what job our relationships are actually hired to do.
What it gives the model The strategic bridge between professional and personal agency — and the credibility of a Harvard Business School voice speaking the same language as Frankl and Seligman. Christensen gives the skeptical executive permission to take the inner work seriously. He also provides the most precise account of how good people end up below the line not through dramatic failure but through the accumulated marginal cost of a thousand small choices.

The Full Model Landscape

Every Leadership Theory,
Grounded in Agency

Each cluster of models rests on a specific application of the agency principle. None of them work without it.

Classical & Foundational
Agency assumption: Leaders choose how they behave, regardless of who they inherently are.

Situational Leadership asks you to consciously choose a different style. Path-Goal asks you to choose to clear obstacles. Transactional Leadership asks you to choose integrity in keeping your side of the exchange. The trait theories are the exception — they assumed agency away. The rest assume it.

↗ Explore this cluster
Transformational & Purpose
Agency assumption: Leaders choose to act beyond self-interest — to serve something larger.

Transformational leadership is the choice to be inspired and inspiring rather than merely transactional. Servant leadership is the daily choice to put others first. Authentic leadership is the choice to align action with values even when it's costly. Purpose-driven leadership is the choice to ask why before what.

↗ Explore this cluster
Psychological & Inner World
Agency assumption: You can choose your response to your own inner states.

Emotional intelligence is agency applied to emotional data. Mindful leadership is agency applied to attention. Neuroleadership shows that understanding threat/reward responses gives you more choice over them. Humble leadership is the ongoing choice to remain open rather than defended.

↗ Explore this cluster
Systems & Adaptive
Agency assumption: You can choose to see the system clearly rather than just react to it.

Adaptive leadership begins with the choice to get on the balcony — to see the dance floor from above. Cynefin asks you to choose the right sense-making frame rather than defaulting to the familiar. Complexity Leadership asks you to choose enabling conditions over control. VUCA leadership is agency under pressure.

↗ Explore this cluster
Relational & Social
Agency assumption: You can choose the quality of your relationships — with each person, every day.

LMX shows that in-group vs. out-group is a choice — often unconscious, but a choice. Followership theory asks followers to choose active engagement rather than passive compliance. Psychological safety is the leader's choice to model vulnerability first. Distributed leadership is the choice to share power rather than hoard it.

↗ Explore this cluster
Cultural, Global & Ethical
Agency assumption: Values are not just felt — they are chosen and enacted under pressure.

Ethical leadership is agency in the face of organizational pressure to compromise. Justice-based leadership is the daily choice to treat people with fairness even when asymmetric power makes it unnecessary. Sustainable leadership is agency extended across time — choosing future generations in present decisions.

↗ Explore this cluster
Emerging & Frontier
Agency assumption: New contexts do not eliminate choice — they reveal it more clearly.

Digital leadership is the choice to lead the human dimensions of technology change, not just deploy tools. Virtual leadership requires the choice to invest in trust-building that co-located teams take for granted. Open leadership is the choice to relinquish control in service of collective intelligence.

The Pedagogy

Why the Wilderness
Teaches This Best

The wilderness does not accept excuses. It does not grade on a curve. It does not reward performance over substance. It is the purest available laboratory for agency — because the consequences of going below the line are immediate, real, and visible to everyone.

🏔
The Summit Decision

The summit pushes the T·F·A loop to its edge. Tired thoughts, fear feelings, depleted bodies. The leader who chooses above the line — for themselves and the group — demonstrates agency in its most elemental form.

Agency in extremis
🌧
Day Four in the Rain

The below-the-line patterns emerge reliably around day four: blame the weather, blame the guide, blame the plan. The wilderness makes these patterns visible to everyone — including the person exhibiting them. This visibility is irreplaceable.

Below the line, unmasked
🔥
The Campfire Debrief

After each day, the group names what happened using the model's language: "I was below the line at the river crossing. Here's what I was thinking. Here's how it felt. Here's what I did — and what I wish I'd done." Agency is taught in the debrief, not just the experience.

Reflection as agency
🧭
When the Map Is Wrong

The map doesn't match the terrain. This is the Adaptive Leadership moment — and the Cynefin moment — and the agency moment. Blame the map (below the line) or own the navigation (above). The group watches. The leader reveals themselves.

Adaptive challenge, live
🌄
The Solo

A period of structured solitude in nature — from hours to a full day. No feedback, no social performance, no professional identity. Just the person, the landscape, and the unmediated experience of their own thoughts, feelings, and the agency that runs between them.

Frankl's space, made literal
🤝
Servant Carry

On the hardest day, the leaders carry extra weight so others can make it. Not symbolically — literally. Servant leadership, humble leadership, inclusive leadership: all become embodied in a single act that costs something real.
